\section{Solution}
We need to solve:

\begin{align}
\int^\infty_{-\infty} \int^\infty_{-\infty} e^{r_{P} + B_{P}(x_P,y_A) - C_{P}(x_P,y_A) - m_{P} P_{t}} N(x_P) N(y_A) dx_P dy_A   
\end{align}

This is a difficult integral. One way to approximate it is to Taylor expand the integrand. In fact, Eq. \ref{pop_dyn_P} contains the zero-th order term by simply replacing $x_P$ and $y_A$ by the plant and animal mean traits. A first order approximation may already be an improvement. \vspace{\baselineskip}

We can derive a beautiful continuous closed-form formula for this Double Integral by performing a Gaussian Integration (also known as Laplace's Method) on the inner exponent.

Because standard fractional exponents aren't integratable, we first apply the second-order Taylor expansion strictly to the exponent $g(x,y)$ inside, leaving it wrapped in the integral: $$ \iint \exp\left(g_0 + g_x(x-\mu_P) + g_y(y-\mu_A) + \frac{1}{2}g_{xx}(x-\mu_P)^2 + \frac{1}{2}g_{yy}(y-\mu_A)^2\right) N(x) N(y) dx dy $$

Because you are multiplying this exponential function against two Normal Distributions ($\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}$), we can combine the $x^2$ polynomials into two uniform Gaussian integrals: $\int e^{-A u^2 + B u} du$.

By mathematically executing the exact Gaussian integrals $\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{A}} e^{\frac{B^2}{4A}}$, the infinite double-integral collapses entirely into this single, exact closed-form algebraic formula:

$$ I_{closed} = \frac{ \exp\left( g(\mu_P, \mu_A) + \frac{g_x^2 \sigma_P^2}{2(1 - g_{xx} \sigma_P^2)} + \frac{g_y^2 \sigma_A^2}{2(1 - g_{yy} \sigma_A^2)} \right) } { \sqrt{1 - g_{xx} \sigma_P^2} \sqrt{1 - g_{yy} \sigma_A^2} } $$

What the Terms Are (in terms of your parameters):
$g(\mu_P, \mu_A)$ = The fitness exponent evaluated at the trait means: $r_P + B_P(\mu_P,\mu_A) - C_P(\mu_P,\mu_A) - m_P P_{t}$.
$g_x$ and $g_y$ = The First-order partial derivatives of your fitness exponent with respect to $x_P$ and $y_A$ (evaluated at the means).
$g_{xx}$ and $g_{yy}$ = The Second-order partial derivatives (Hessian components) of your fitness exponent with respect to $x_P$ and $y_A$ (evaluated at the means).
$\sigma_P^2$ and $\sigma_A^2$ = The trait variances of the specific Plant and Animal species.

\section{Calculation of the Partial Derivatives}
To evaluate the exact Laplace algebraic formula, we must calculate the partial derivatives of $g(x_P, y_A)$ evaluated symmetrically at the means $(\mu_P, \mu_A)$. The exponent is defined as $g(x_P, y_A) = r_P - m_P P_t + B_P(x_P, y_A) - C_P(x_P, y_A)$.

Assuming the trait means do not perfectly identically overlap ($\mu_P \neq \mu_A$), the derivatives of the Heaviside step function $H(y_A - x_P)$ evaluate exactly to the Dirac delta $\delta(\mu_A - \mu_P) = 0$. Thus, all derivative bounds of the cost fraction are identically zero at the expansion center: $C_x = C_y = C_{xx} = C_{yy} = 0$.

The benefit function relies on the active interaction fraction $M_P(x, y) = \frac{A_t}{P_t} \frac{f(x-y)}{f(\mu_P - y)}$, where $f(x-y) = \exp\left(-\frac{(x-y)^2}{2\sigma_c^2}\right)$. Let $d = \mu_P - \mu_A$. By applying the quotient and logarithmic derivative rules evaluated at $(\mu_P, \mu_A)$, we find that both the first and second derivatives with respect to the animal trait identically cancel to zero ($M_y = 0$, $M_{yy} = 0$) because an animal's deviation from its own species mean naturally does not impact the relative overlap fraction $f / f$ for a flower sitting exactly at its own mean. 

The plant trait gradient, however, remains actively pressured:
\begin{align}
    \left. M \right|_{\mu} &= \frac{A_t}{P_t} \\
    \left. M_x \right|_{\mu} &= -\frac{A_t}{P_t} \frac{d}{\sigma_c^2} \\
    \left. M_{xx} \right|_{\mu} &= \frac{A_t}{P_t} \frac{d^2 - \sigma_c^2}{\sigma_c^4}
\end{align}

We apply these fractions directly to the Monod growth function $B_P(M) = b_P \frac{M}{M + M_{P0}}$, taking its algebraic derivatives $B'(M) = b_P \frac{M_{P0}}{(M + M_{P0})^2}$ and $B''(M) = -2b_P \frac{M_{P0}}{(M + M_{P0})^3}$. This yields the final explicit partial derivatives for the closed form integral:

\begin{align}
    g_x &= B'(M) M_x = b_P \frac{M_{P0}}{(\frac{A_t}{P_t} + M_{P0})^2} \left( -\frac{A_t}{P_t} \frac{\mu_P - \mu_A}{\sigma_c^2} \right) \\
    g_{xx} &= B''(M) (M_x)^2 + B'(M) M_{xx} \\
    g_y &= g_{yy} = 0 
\end{align}
Because $g_y$ and $g_{yy}$ mathematically evaluate to $0$, the animal variance $\sigma_A^2$ algebra collapses securely out of the formula entirely!

\subsection*{Generalization to Multi-Species Competition}
When scaling from a 1-to-1 ecosystem to a generalized multi-species network ($S_P > 1$, $S_A > 1$), the community denominator interactions ($\sum P_{t,k} f_{k,j}$) prevent the biological traits from cleanly canceling identically out. 

Let $D_j = \sum_{k=1}^{S_P} P_{t,k} f(\mu_{P_k} - \mu_{A_j})$ represent the total competitive availability array of animal $j$, and $d_{i,j} = \mu_{P_i} - \mu_{A_j}$ be the trait distance. The fractional access $M_{i,j} = \frac{A_{t,j}}{D_j} f(d_{i,j})$ now inherently depends competitively on the surrounding community traits.

Evaluating the partial derivatives of $M$ with respect to the plant trait $x_i$ and animal trait $y_j$ at the community means reveals a profound structural relationship:
\begin{align}
    \left. M_{x_i} \right|_{\mu} &= M_{i,j} \left( -\frac{d_{i,j}}{\sigma_c^2} \right) \\
    \left. M_{x_i x_i} \right|_{\mu} &= M_{i,j} \frac{d_{i,j}^2 - \sigma_c^2}{\sigma_c^4} 
\end{align}
For the corresponding animal trait, let $\bar{d}_j = \frac{\sum_k P_{t,k} f_{k,j} d_{k,j}}{D_j}$ be the competitive abundance-weighted average trait distance of the entire community accessed by animal $j$. By computing $\frac{\partial}{\partial y_j} (\frac{N}{D})$ via the quotient rule, we find:
\begin{align}
    \left. M_{y_j} \right|_{\mu} &= \frac{M_{i,j}}{\sigma_c^2} \left( d_{i,j} - \bar{d}_j \right) \\
    \left. M_{y_j y_j} \right|_{\mu} &= M_{i,j} \left[ \left(\frac{d_{i,j} - \bar{d}_j}{\sigma_c^2}\right)^2 - \frac{\Delta \text{Var}_D(d_j)}{\sigma_c^4} \right]
\end{align}
Where $\Delta \text{Var}_D(d_j) = \overline{d_{j}^2} - (\bar{d}_j)^2$ is the variance of the competing community's mathematical trait distances.

\textbf{Biological Proof:} Because the animal trait gradient $g_y$ depends directly on $M_y$, we mathematically prove that an animal's variance ($\sigma_A^2$) \textit{only exerts fitness pressure on a plant} ($g_{yy} \neq 0$) if there are multiple actively competing plants pushing the weighted community average $\bar{d}_j$ away from the focal plant $d_{i,j}$. In a 1-to-1 system, $\Delta \text{Var}_D$ evaluates to 0 and inherently $d_i = \bar{d}_j$, proving algebraically why our previous isolated system collapsed perfectly to $g_y = g_{yy} = 0$!

By substituting $M_i = \sum_{j} M_{i,j}$ upward into $B'(M)$ and $B''(M)$, we completely recover the exact multi-species algebraic components mapping into Laplace's expanded continuous integration matrix!


\section{Alternative Approach: The Mean-Field Integration}
Is it better to add more terms to the Taylor Expansion or Laplace formula? No. Adding more polynomial terms (3rd or 4th order, representing mathematical skewness and kurtosis matrices) actually actively degrades the approximation because discontinuous step functions intrinsically lack a stable Taylor radius of convergence.

Instead, a geometrically stable alternative can be constructed via the \textbf{Mean-Field Cumulant Expansion}. Rather than enveloping the entire exponential function in a multivariate outer integral, we legally pull the Expected Value integrals \textit{inside} the exponential prior to algebraic distribution:
$$ \langle W_P \rangle \approx \exp\left( r_P - m_P P_t + \langle B_P \rangle - \langle C_P \rangle \right) $$

By integrating the fitness fractions directly against the biological trait variance equations defined in the paper matrix, the Heaviside $H$ and fractional $f$ equations resolve cleanly and exactly into the smooth standard Normal CDF forms ($\Phi$) designated in Equations 119 and 141 ($H_{int}$ and $f_{int}$):

$$ \langle B_P \rangle \approx b_P \frac{f_{int}(\mu_P, \mu_A)}{f_{int}(\mu_P, \mu_A) + \frac{M_{P0}}{A_t}} $$

Because the traits algebraically cancel each other cleanly out of the competition models across a uniform cross-species population, this provides an irrefutable, fully-parameterized simulation framework that completely bypasses the intractable double integral without relying on polynomial estimations.

\section{Fully Expanded Algebraic Solutions}
To prevent relying on sequential partial-derivative substitution components, here is the absolute integration mathematical closure resolved as a single explicit ``giant formula'' for both analytical approaches in the generalized 1-to-1 paradigm. Let $K = \frac{A_t}{P_t}$ and $d = \mu_P - \mu_A$.
